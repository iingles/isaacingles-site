Once upon a time, a little boy went to play in the forest. His mother and father told him not to go too far, but he didn't listen to them, and soon found himself hopelessly lost. The shadows formed shapes on the ground that frightened him, and he imagined large beasts on the other sides of the trees, waiting to eat him up. The boy called for help, and began to cry until he heard a voice behind him.

“Why are you crying?” the voice said. The boy turned around and saw a child, about his age, staring at him from a tree branch above. Still crying, the boy said, “I didn't obey my parents, and now I'm lost in the forest and don't know how to get home!”

“Do not fear,” the tree-child said. “I know the way back to the village. I will take you home.”

So the tree-child led the boy home. By the time they reached the boy's house, it was already dark. The boy's parents embraced him, crying, at the same time chastising him for disobeying them. The boy introduced his parents to the tree-child, saying, “If it was not for him, I might have never made it home”. The boy's parents showered the tree-child with praise, and when the tree-child turned to go, the boy's father said, “It's dark outside, and your journey will be dangerous. Come, stay here for the night, and you may leave in the morning.”

The tree-child agreed, and the boy's mother prepared a mat and blanket for him to sleep, next to the boy's bed. And so they slept until the following morning.

The boy arose, and found the tree-child sleeping on the floor. The boy was overjoyed, and woke the tree-child. The two of them played games together, some the tree-child showed the boy, and they became the best of friends. The boy's parents watched the two play and smiled as they rested on a bench in front of their house.

Later, the father had his son and the tree-child help him load some wood into a cart after he chopped it. As the father was chopping the wood, the two children began to play with each other once again. The father chastised the children for playing around him while he was chopping wood, aware of the dangerous ax that he wielded. But the children did not listen, and sure enough, the tree-child's finger became caught on the chopping block just as the father's ax came down on top of it.

The tree-child screamed in pain and withdrew his hand. There was a deep cut. The father stared curiously at the liquid that had spilled from the tree-child's hand. Where there should have been blood, there was a thick, brownish liquid. The boy helped his friend wrap his injured hand while they both ran inside the house to the mother. The father continued to stare at the liquid, and gathered some on his finger. Curiously, he moved the cut end of his finger to his nose, and sniffed. It smelled delicious. His inhibitions gone, he stuck out his tongue and tasted the end of his finger. Chocolate! And not just any chocolate, but the most delicious chocolate in the whole wide world! Suddenly ravenous and unable to control himself, the father found himself licking up the rest of the spattered chocolate from the chopping block.

The boy and the tree-child ran into the house, where the mother was cooking. Both children cried as they told mother what had happened. Mother tenderly took the tree-child into her arms and unwrapped his hand to look at the wound. As she cleaned it, she too noticed the brown fluid, not blood, seeping out. Some dribbled off onto her hand as she dressed the tree-child's hand. She smelled the sweetness of the chocolate, and, like the Father, she could not hold herself back from tasting it. As she withdrew her hand from her mouth, she looked up at the children, both of whom were staring at her, horrified. Suddenly, Father burst into the room, looking past his son to the tree-child. He wielded his axe and smiled. The tree-child was frozen in fear, but the boy took him by the hand and led him running out the back door, away from the house and towards the village.

They ran into the villiage bakery and the boy said, "Please, sir! We need a place to hide! My mother and father have gone mad, and now they're after us!" The baker thought a moment, then decided to let them hide in his kitchen. When the Mother and Father had passed, the cook went into the kitchen where the children were hiding and said, "Now what was that all about?" The boy said, "My mother and father tasted the blood of this tree-child, and now they want to eat him!" The baker put his hand to his chin, and thought a moment. Business had been slow, and everybody in the town had been suffering. If this tree-child possessed something so de-lectable flowing through his veins, it might just make him a richer man. So the baker told the children, "Hide in here, " opening his oven, "just in case they come back - I'll be sure to tell you when it's safe!"

But the children knew that the baker didn't really want to help them. They backed towards the exit. The baker quickly cut them off, saying, "Where do you think you're going?". The tree-child and the boy thought they were trapped, when Mother and Father burst through the door,knocking the baker over. While the three lie tangled on the floor, the children ran around them, out of the bakery, and into the town square. There they met a policeman. "Help, help, sir!" said the boy. The policeman said, "What is the matter?" The boy replied, "My mother and father want to eat my friend, and so does the baker!" The policeman thought that was very odd, but he took them to talk to the Mayor. "Mayor, sir, my mother and father and the baker are trying to catch us, because they want to eat my friend!". The mayor thought a moment, then said, "Why would they want to eat your friend?" "They tasted this..." said the boy, holding up the tree-child's
wounded hand, which was still dribbling delicious chocolate. Both the mayor and the policeman caught the smell; it danced into their noses like the song of a siren; and they were drawn towards the tree-child. The two children ran, and hid behind a row of houses. They could hear voices, angry voices of more people who had heard of the chocolate-bearing tree-child, and were searching for him.

This made the boy afraid; but curious at the same time. As the tree-child looked around a corner, away from the boy, he noticed the cut in the tree-child's hand. He leaned in closer to smell, and was overcome by the delicious aroma. His inhibition suddenly gone, he lunged forward into his friend and took a delicious chocolate bite out of his shoulder. The tree-child screamed in pain; and the boy lunged after him again, hungry for more. The tree-child ran into the street, where he was spotted by several more people. They ran after him. The tree-child spotted the edge of the city, beyond which was his forest; and ran towards it. It felt like he was going to make it, yes, he was free! But suddenly he was scooped up by a swift hunter on horseback, and carried into the town square. The tree-child was tied to a table, and the townspeople gathered round, each of them eager for a bite. The tree-child spotted the boy amongst the crowd, and
shouted; "Save me! save me, please! I saved your life!" But the boy would not listen. The crowd descended upon the tree-child, and he was no more.

And thus, after the sweetest human treasure in the world, life, had been preserved by one; it was taken away by others who were so greedy that they cared more about the temporal sweetness than the spiritual sweetness; although it can still be said that with this story, true beauty always lies within.
